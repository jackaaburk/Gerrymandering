Solutions to Gerrymandering

Practical solutions do exist for a problem as big as gerrymandering.

One way to aid the problem of gerrymandering is to have independent redistricting commissions or IRCs.

States like Arizona and Michigan already use IRCs for redistricting and rarely have unfair maps.

IRCs usually have an even number of Republicans, Democrats, and Independent voters. They hold hearings and create maps based on practical statistics like compactness, population, and age, excluding partisan statistics.

In current times, computers have the capability to draw completely fair and neutral district maps, allowing for precise mapping without the consideration of partisanship statistics.

Furthermore, these programs that the computers could use could be made open-source, which means anyone can see them and how they work. This means that it has complete transparency and there is no corrupt districting going on.

Congress has the complete ability to pass a law that effectively bans partisan gerrymandering.

This would allow the Supreme Court to oversee possible cases of partisan gerrymanders and rule them unconstitutional if need be.

Furthermore, Congress could instead require the use of IRCs in every state, offering another solution.

Bills like the For the People Act that aim to end partisan gerrymandering have been proposed but have died in the Senate.
