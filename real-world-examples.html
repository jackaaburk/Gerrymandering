Real-World Examples

Gerrymandering is a bipartisan problem. Depending on which party controls the state legislature during a redistricting year, maps are often drawn to maximize that party's power. Here are some of the most prominent examples from recent history.

Before: Democrats had 13 seats, and Republicans had 5. The vote was 66% Democrats and 34% Republicans.

After: Democrats had 14 seats, and Republicans had 3. The vote was 54% Democrats and 56% Republicans.

Effect: With this gerrymandering, Republicans living in Illinois are hugely suppressed. While the percentage of voters is roughly the same, they only have 17% of the seats in the House. This is a disparity of 39%, which is roughly 1,694,000 people. This effect is huge on people's voices in the government.

Before: Democrats had 34 seats, and Republicans had 19. The vote was 53% Democrats and 47% Republicans.

After: Democrats had 40 seats, and Republicans had 12. The vote was 63% Democrats and 37% Republicans.

Effect: With this gerrymandering, Republicans living in California had their voices greatly reduced. Their former 35% of seats in the House of Representatives was reduced to 23%. While that does not sound like a lot, 11% of the Republican voters is roughly 635,000 people.

Before: Democrats had 52 seats, and Republicans had 46. The vote was 55% Democrats and 45% Republicans.

After: Democrats had 38 seats, and Republicans had 60. The vote was 43% Democrats and 57% Republicans.

Effect: Despite having almost similar vote percentages, the Republicans held 61% of the seats. Due to this, in 2012, when the vote was was 53% Democrats and 47% Republicans, the Republicans still held 61% of the seats, which is clearly not proportional to the vote.
