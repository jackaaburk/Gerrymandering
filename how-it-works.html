Cracking is the practice of drawing electoral districts that divide the population of a community or constituency across several districts. This process basically reduces the influence of the community or constituency, preventing them from voting and having a majority in any given district.

Packing refers to the practice of drawing electoral districts to consolidate the population of a community or constituency into a small number of districts. By packing a majority of the community or constituency into a small number of districts, the opposing party can win more seats than they otherwise would, while the community or constituency ends up with fewer seats.

Key Strategy: Effective gerrymanders usually combine packing and cracking. The mapmaker "packs" the opposition into a few districts they concede, and "cracks" the rest of the opposition's voters across many districts where they will safely lose.

A wasted vote is any vote cast that does not go to the election winner, and thus, the voter is not represented in the outcome. Additionally, it is any vote for the election winner that was not needed to win the election.

The efficiency gap is a standard for measuring partisan gerrymandering. It is a formula that calculates the difference between the two parties’ wasted votes, divided by the total number of votes. A higher efficiency gap indicates a partisan gerrymander.

Courts and reformers have identified several criteria that neutral, fair maps should follow:

  • Population Equality: There is population equality between a state’s congressional districts.
  • Compactness and Contiguity: A compact district represents a geographically consolidated area, and contiguous means that one can travel anywhere in a district without crossing over into another district.
  • Racial and Language Minority Protections: States can not draw district maps that have the effect of reducing or diluting minority voting strength.
  • Preservation of Communities of Interest: Districts should keep together communities that share common political, social, or economic interests.
Key Takeaway: Gerrymandering typically violates the spirit of compactness and community preservation, even when it technically satisfies contiguity and equal population requirements.
