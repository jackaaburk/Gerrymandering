History of Gerrymandering

Gerrymandering is as old as America. In the first congressional map drawn in 1788, Anti-Federalists forced Federalist candidates into the same seats as Anti-Federalist candidates in hopes of lowering their chances of winning. The term was coined in 1812 in the Boston Gazette, with an image of a salamander in place of one of Massachusetts's districts to highlight its strange shape.

The Constitution has no specific method or system for the states to draw congressional districts. Thus, the current voting scheme was created as if a particular party were more popular in a certain state. If there were a general popular vote without districts, then the opposing party would get no seats, but with districts, they still may get some.

There were constant switches between statewide districts and single-member districts until Congress passed the Apportionment Act of 1842, forcing the use of single-member districts. With this act passed, many states at the time, whose majority party was more advantaged to use statewide districts, held out on complying. As states could not shift back and forth between the two methods, they now attempted to maximize their advantages in the single-member districts. During this era, the Apportionment Act of 1872 added a requirement that districts must contain contiguous territory.

However, these requirements were often ignored and eventually lapsed or were repealed, leading to decades of unequal and malapportioned districts.

The most aggressive era of gerrymandering was 1878-1896.

During this era it there was a large Republican dominance, and there was a large decline in competitive states. The North and South solidified as Republican and Democrat, respectively, and district boundaries became more stable. Additionally, the House of Representatives stopped expanding. With districts solidifying, they changed little over time, but due to people moving to cities, they became unbalanced.

The Supreme Court made a decision that each person is one vote, meaning the states had to redraw their districts to make them more evenly distributed, thus starting the “Reapportionment Revolution.” Additionally, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 included sections to protect minority rights, including protection from racial gerrymandering. District competition has declined and is continuing to decline. However, as of late, computer models are being used to redraw district maps in favor of a certain party with unprecedented precision, thus spiking the increase in use of Gerrymandering. Additionally, there has been an increase in mid-year drawings of Congressional districts, starting with Texas and California.
