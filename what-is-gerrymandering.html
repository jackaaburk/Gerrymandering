What is Gerrymandering?

The term Gerrymandering is a combination of a 19th-century political figure and a mythological salamander. It was coined in the Boston Gazette in an article reacting to the newly drawn districts of Massachusetts. These districts were signed into effect by Elbridge Gerry, and one of the districts looked like a mythological salamander; the two were put together, creating Gerrymandering.

"Gerry was a signer of the Declaration of Independence, a two-term member of the House of Representatives, governor of Massachusetts and U.S. vice president under James Madison. His name, however, was forever negatively linked to this form of political powerbroking."

Gerrymandering, according to Merriam-Webster, is the practice of dividing or arranging a territorial unit into election districts in a way that gives one political party an unfair advantage in elections. Simply put, it is the way politicians redraw congressional districts to give a particular group of people a more favorable district, so they can win more votes overall, even if it is not proportional to the beliefs of all voters in the state.

While redistricting is a normal, required process that happens every 10 years to adjust for population changes, gerrymandering is the abuse of this process.

  • Partisan Gerrymandering: The political party in power redraws maps to maximize its own seats while minimizing the other parties' seats.
  • Racial/Ethnic Gerrymandering: Districts are drawn to lower or unfairly raise a particular racial or ethnic group's voting power.
  • Incumbent Gerrymandering: The two parties agree to protect incumbents from both parties and not to redraw the map so that they lose reelection.
  • Redistricting: The process of redrawing legislative district boundaries, required every 10 years after the census.
  • District: A geographic area represented by one elected official.
  • Packing: Concentrating as many opposing party or minority voters as possible into a few districts.
  • Cracking: Spreading disfavored voters thinly across multiple districts to dilute their vote.
Key Takeaway: Redistricting itself is a normal, constitutional process. Gerrymandering is its abuse for partisan or racial advantage.
